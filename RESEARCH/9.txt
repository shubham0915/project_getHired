I went through this the hard way after we realized “just trust the provider” falls apart once support logs, retries, and prompts start piling up. What worked for us was stripping obvious PII before the model call, then replacing it with stable placeholders so the app could rehydrate later if needed. Client-side filtering sounded nice, but I found server-side was easier to audit and way less fragile across web and voice flows.

We also split prompts into safe context and sensitive lookups, so the model only got what it actually needed. I tried Presidio for detection and ended up using Azure’s PII detection in a few flows, but we switched to DreamFactory after testing both plus custom middleware because it gave us a cleaner gate in front of internal data and stopped a bunch of accidental overexposure from ad hoc connectors. Biggest lesson was logging the redacted payload, not the raw one, or your whole setup is fake.

Local pre-processing is the only way to be sure, you cant trust the API to forget what you sent it.

We built a tiny model called micro-f1-mask to act as a surgical guardrail. It redacts or masks PII at the edge before the prompt ever hits the external LLM. It’s faster than using a large model for pre-processing and more reliable than regex.

Weights and scripts are open if you need to tune it for your specific compliance stack:
https://github.com/ARPAHLS/micro-f1-mask

Use a tool that can identify PII, tokenize it before it hits the LLM. This was context is completely maintained and the LLM is not reading a bunch of [redacted]. Get the response and the tool reverses the tokenized data.

It’s been pretty simple to use, especially since we didn’t want to do a lot of heavy lifting.
